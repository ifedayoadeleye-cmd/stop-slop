# Before/After Examples

## Example 1: Throat-Clearing + Binary Contrast

**Before:**
> "Here's the thing: building products is hard. Not because the technology is complex. Because people are complex. Let that sink in."

**After:**
> "Building products is hard. Technology is manageable. People aren't."

**Changes:** Removed opener, binary contrast structure, and emphasis crutch. Direct statements.

---

## Example 2: Filler + Unnecessary Reassurance

**Before:**
> "It turns out that most teams struggle with alignment. The uncomfortable truth is that nobody wants to admit they're confused. And that's okay."

**After:**
> "Teams struggle with alignment. Nobody admits confusion."

**Changes:** Cut hedging ("most"), removed throat-clearing phrases, deleted permission-granting ending.

---

## Example 3: Business Jargon Stack

**Before:**
> "In today's fast-paced landscape, we need to lean into discomfort and navigate uncertainty with clarity. This matters because your competition isn't waiting."

**After:**
> "Move faster. Your competition is."

**Changes:** Eliminated jargon entirely. Core message in six words.

---

## Example 4: Dramatic Fragmentation

**Before:**
> "Speed. Quality. Cost. You can only pick two. That's it. That's the tradeoff."

**After:**
> "Speed, quality, cost: pick two."

**Changes:** Single sentence. No performative emphasis.

---

## Example 5: Rhetorical Setup

**Before:**
> "What if I told you that the best teams don't optimize for productivity? Here's what I mean: they optimize for learning. Think about it."

**After:**
> "The best teams optimize for learning, not productivity."

**Changes:** Direct claim. No rhetorical scaffolding.

---

## Example 6: Complexity Signalling

**Before:**
> "The reality is more nuanced than it appears. There are no easy answers here. This is a genuinely complex issue that deserves more attention than it gets."

**After:**
> "Three forces pull in opposite directions: shareholder return, regulatory compliance, and the interests of employees who weren't consulted. None yields easily."

**Changes:** Removed complexity announcement. Replaced with the actual complexity.

---

## Example 7: Empathy Performance

**Before:**
> "Changing careers is hard. This can feel overwhelming, and that's completely normal. You're not alone in feeling this way. Be patient with yourself as you navigate this transition."

**After:**
> "Most people changing careers underestimate the middle period, the six to twelve months when the old identity has dissolved and the new one hasn't formed yet. That gap is the work."

**Changes:** Cut the generic reassurance. Named the specific hard thing instead.

---

## Example 8: Discovery Narration

**Before:**
> "As I researched this further, I found something surprising. The more I dug into the data, the more I realised that the conventional wisdom was wrong. What I found challenged everything I thought I knew."

**After:**
> "The conventional wisdom is wrong. Remote teams that overlap by two hours daily outperform those with full schedule alignment by 23% on complex problem-solving tasks."

**Changes:** Removed the journey to the finding. Led with the finding. Added specifics.

---

## Example 9: Hedged Urgency

**Before:**
> "This may be one of the most important decisions your organisation will face. What might be a turning point for the industry could also be potentially transformative for how you operate."

**After:**
> "This decision will determine whether you compete in the next market cycle or watch from the outside."

**Changes:** Cut the modal hedges. Made a direct claim about the specific consequence.

---

## Example 10: Transition Glue

**Before:**
> "Remote teams struggle with async communication. With that in mind, tooling becomes critical. Against this backdrop, companies are investing heavily in documentation culture. Taken together, these trends point to a fundamental shift in how work gets done."

**After:**
> "Remote teams that invest in documentation outperform those that rely on real-time communication for complex decisions. The tooling follows the practice, not the other way around."

**Changes:** Removed all transition phrases. Merged four weak sentences into two that actually connect causally.

---

## Example 11: Enumerated Non-List

**Before:**
> "There are three things to understand about pricing strategy:
> 1. Value perception matters more than cost-plus calculation.
> 2. Anchoring shapes how buyers read your number.
> 3. Consistency signals confidence."

**After:**
> "Price communicates value before the buyer reads a single feature. Set it too low and you signal doubt; set it relative to an anchor and you control how the number lands. Consistency across channels confirms you believe the price."

**Changes:** Three disconnected items that connect causally became continuous argument. The causal chain was invisible in list form.

---

## Example 12: Weak Verb Construction

**Before:**
> "We need to work to ensure that our teams are able to begin to understand the new process. Leadership should seek to make an effort to take steps to address any confusion."

**After:**
> "Teach the new process before rollout. Leaders handle confusion directly."

**Changes:** Collapsed weak verb chains to single strong verbs. Cut word count by two-thirds.

---

## Example 13: The Wisdom Sandwich

**Before:**
> "Clarity is not a style choice. It's a survival strategy. When teams can't read each other's intent, decisions slow down, trust erodes, and projects stall. Ambiguity isn't neutral. It compounds."

**After:**
> "Teams that can't read each other's intent make slower decisions and revisit the same ground repeatedly. The cost is measurable: one extra alignment meeting per week per team adds up to three weeks of lost time annually. Most of that time goes to recovering from misread signals, not resolving genuine disagreement."

**Changes:** Removed the aphorism frame at both ends. Replaced with the specific mechanism and a number that earns the claim.

---

## Example 14: The Summary That Restates

**Before:**
> [Opening:] "Most feedback fails because it arrives too late to be useful."
> [Closing:] "Ultimately, the core issue with feedback is timing. When it comes too late, it can't change anything. That's why getting feedback right, and getting it early, is so important."

**After:**
> [Closing replaced with:] "The teams that get this right treat feedback as part of the work, not a review of it. They build the checkpoint into the process so the conversation happens while the decision is still live."

**Changes:** Closing paragraph now adds the mechanism (embed it in process) rather than restating the opening claim (timing matters).
